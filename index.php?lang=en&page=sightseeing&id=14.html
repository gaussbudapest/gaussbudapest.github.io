14. Heroes’ square

Individual authors have called the Heroes’ Square in many different ways: ’square of the country’, ’square of ceremonial occasions’ or ’the open-air stage of Hungarian history’. In fact, each name expresses a segment of reality that is symbolized by each building in the square. The establishment of Heroes’ Square is associated with the millenial celebration held in 1896, when the Parliament agreed that an impressive monument should be erected to the memory of Hungarian conquest and also to immortalize the past thousand years. György Zala, and Albert Schickedanz were charged with designing the monument to a well-chosen place. Work on construction projects was progressing rather slowly. The statue of Gabriel archangel, holding the Hungarian crown on his right and a cross in his left hand, was first done, and then the sculptures of kings as well as those of the seven leaders followed. The bronzes spaced onto the four pillars of the colonnade allegorically symbolize war and peace, plus science and art.

Heroes' Square often gives place to mass events, several demonstrations and political congresses. The lay out before the reburial of Imre Nagy and his co-martyrs took place here on 16 June 1989. On 20 August 1991, Pope John Paul II. pontificated a holy mass on the square, which was attended by about 200 000 people. National Gallop has been held here annually since 2008.

The Heroes' Square is the most spacious and most impressive square of Budapest, which is located in the XIVth district. In a narrower sense it refers to the Millennium Monument. Heroes' Square and the Andrássy Avenue, which lies in front of it, are World Heritage Sites.
